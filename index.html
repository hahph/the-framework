01

Business Challenges

At 16% annual attrition — above the benchmark — the organization was losing talent faster than it could retain.
Yet the more pressing problem was not the rate itself, it was that no one could explain what was driving it.

Talent Sustainability Gap

Attrition Without a Traceable Cause

Turnover remained above benchmark, yet no mechanism existed to determine whether it was driven by structural misalignment, compensation gaps, career ceiling, or market pull. Without distinguishing the root cause, any intervention risked treating the symptom.

Risk: Misdiagnosing an attrition driver doesn't just waste retention spend — it accelerates the erosion of institutional knowledge while weakening employer brand

Diagnostic Segmentation Gap

No Profile of Who Is Leaving

Aggregate attrition figures were never broken down by segments — leaving the organization unable to determine whether departures were evenly distributed or clustered within a group of employees whose loss carried outsized impact.

Risk: Retention resources spread uniformly across the workforce protect everyone in theory — but a concentrated problem demands a targeted response

HR Capacity Gap

The Solving Team Is Breaking Down

As annual departures across the organization exceeded 700, the HR department faced mounting recruitment and onboarding demand — while simultaneously experiencing its own elevated attrition at 30%, the highest rate across all departments.

Risk: The function responsible for stabilizing workforce turnover was itself in crisis, the organization's capacity to recover would be structurally compromised

Executive Decision Gap

Data Present, Informed Decisions Absent

Workforce data existed across systems, but no analytical framework synthesized it into a coherent picture of underlying causes. The gap between raw data and actionable insights remained unbridged — leaving leadership without a solid basis for decision-making.

Risk: The data remains descriptive rather than prescriptive, and retention strategies default to intuition rather than evidence

02

My Role & Ownership

Sole Analyst & Report Author — End-to-End Analytical Product Development
Approached a real-world workforce dataset as a business problem to be solved — framing the analytical questions, designing the reporting structure, building the dashboard through multiple iterations, and presenting the findings as a decision-ready narrative.

01 / Diagnosis

Problem Structuring

Reviewed the dataset against the organization's stated objective — reducing attrition — and translated it into a set of analytical questions: what is driving departures, which segments are most at risk, and what would it take to make the findings actionable for leadership.

02 / Plan

Analytical Blueprint

Defined the reporting structure before building: key stakeholders, expected output that would answer each question, analysis framework (metrics and dimensions) that helps clarify root cause, a mockup that show the structural report.

03 / Design

Data Preparation

Connected the dataset to Power BI, cleaned data with Power Query, built the data model and DAX measures needed for segment-level analysis, then developed each report page — using visual types selected for analytical clarity rather than aesthetic variety.

04 / Investigate

Insight Development

Conducted the full analytical sequence: from surface patterns (volume, department, level) to cross-dimensional analysis (satisfaction vs. retention, compensation vs. tenure) — identifying the structural misalignment that prior analyses had missed.

05 / Frame

Executive Communication Design

Structured findings into a narrative arc designed for C-suite consumption — sequencing the story from symptom to root cause to intervention, with an executive summary formatted for a sub-two-minute read.

06 / Validate

Financial Rigor

Stress-tested all projections against conservative assumptions — walking back initial ROI estimates from an optimistic 129% to a defensible 37.7%, with full documentation of calculation basis and phase-specific cost trajectories.

Analytical Framework

The core analytical principle — follow the evidence, not the hypothesis.
Each layer of the framework was designed to either confirm or disconfirm the layer before it, separating structural causes from behavioral symptoms before any intervention was recommended.

The Dashboard

Built on Power BI, the report connects every analytical layer into a single navigable system —
from the CEO's 60-second executive brief to the point Finance can trace a departure cohort to its structural origin.

01 / Executive Layer
The 60-Second Brief

Designed for CEO and Board consumption. Four scorecards anchor the investment case at a glance — crisis cost, root cause count, required investment, and projected return. Below, the challenge, key findings, and 3-phase recommendations are formatted to strict line-length discipline: each line readable without re-reading.

Page 01 — Executive Summary
02 / Diagnostic Layer
Workforce Profile

Five visualizations establish the systemic context: satisfaction scores uniformly below threshold; a tenure cliff at the 2–3 year mark; a bimodal experience distribution revealing strong attraction but weak retention; flat working hours across all age cohorts — disproving overtime as a causal factor; and compensation stagnation across every tenure group.

Page 02 — Workforce Profile
03 / Volume Layer
Hot Spot Mapping

A progressive analytical funnel: 711 total departures → 654 in R&D and Sales (92%) → 510 at Level 1–2 (78%) → 312 across three specific roles (44%). Each filter narrows the intervention target from company-wide to surgical — enabling retention spend to be concentrated where return per dollar is highest.

Page 03 — Volume Analysis
04 / Rate Layer
The Dead Angle

HR's reported 30% attrition rate examined through a role-level breakdown: of 189 employees classified under HR, only three hold actual HR roles — with 0% attrition. The "HR crisis" was a data artifact, not an organizational failure. Multi-dimensional analysis and a Sankey diagram of stock option gaps complete the rate-level picture.

Page 04 — Rate Analysis
05 / Structural Layer
Root Cause

The analytical conclusion. A Sankey diagram maps 711 employees from their current misclassified departments to their correct functional assignments — making the structural misalignment visible in a single view. Side-by-side composition charts show how attrition rates normalize post-realignment: HR from 30% to 15%, through org architecture alone.

Page 05 — Root Cause
06 / Decision Layer
Intervention Roadmap

The action-oriented close. A phased roadmap sequences three intervention stages — from stabilization (16%→12%) to consolidation (12%→10%) to excellence (10%→8%) — each with defined initiatives and cost inputs. A seven-KPI success metrics table tracks progress across baseline, Month 6, Month 12, and Month 24.

05

Business Value Delivered

The report created four distinct decision-support capabilities with an evidence-based intervention framework — each addressing a gap that had previously made informed retention action impossible.

✔ Diagnostic Clarity

Root Cause Identified. Interventions Redirected.

Structural Finding — Organizational misalignment was identified as the primary attrition driver: 711 employees in misclassified roles, generating phantom metrics that had misdirected retention efforts for years.

Immediate Corrective Action — An 8-week functional realignment was scoped as the first intervention — requiring no new headcount and projected to halve HR's reported attrition rate immediately upon execution.

✔ Intervention Precision

44% of Attrition. 7% of Workforce. One Cohort.

Targeted Segmentation — 312 employees across three roles, two departments, and a defined tenure window were isolated as the highest-risk cohort — enabling retention investment to be concentrated rather than distributed.

ROI Efficiency — Targeted interventions for this cohort were projected to generate 3–4× the return of equivalent company-wide programs, with $2.8M in salary corrections estimated to recover $27M in avoided turnover cost.

✔ Financial Defensibility

From Optimistic Projection to Board-Ready Case

Conservative Framing — Initial ROI projections were stress-tested and revised downward — from 129% to a defensible 37.7% — with full documentation of calculation basis, phase-specific cost trajectories, and contingency buffers.

CFO-Ready Model — The $22M investment case and $31M net savings projection were structured to withstand line-by-line scrutiny, providing leadership with a number they could defend rather than one they would need to qualify.

✔ Decision Infrastructure

A Monitoring Framework, Not Just a Recommendation

Ongoing Visibility — The dashboard's seven-KPI success metrics table gave leadership a structured tracking layer — translating a one-time analysis into an operational monitoring tool for the full 24-month intervention period.

Adaptive Management — Phase-gated milestones with defined go/no-go criteria enabled leadership to adjust investment pace based on actual results, rather than committing to a fixed plan against an uncertain execution environment.
